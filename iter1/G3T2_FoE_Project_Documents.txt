Project Title: Focus or Else (FoE)
Course: CS206 Software Product Management
Group: G3T2
Members: Clemira Euphemie Iverem Jenkins, Kevin Johannes Saputra, Daniella Setio, Andrew Nathanael Murty, Matthew Wilson, Ling Sing Ho

HEADLINE / ELEVATOR PITCH
Focus or Else (FoE) is a social accountability app that helps students follow through on their goals by pairing commitment with fair, pre-agreed consequences. By turning responsibility into a shared game rather than a solo struggle, FoE helps students build discipline and develop healthier, more consistent habits.

PROBLEM WORTH SOLVING
Most students start their semester with high ambitions, but good intentions often crumble when faced with the daily friction of procrastination. While productivity apps exist, they typically rely on self-discipline — which is exactly what students are struggling with in the first place.

Problems the app aims to address:

1. The "Solo Struggle" Trap
Existing habit trackers are private. When a student fails a goal in isolation, there are zero stakes and no one to notice, making it easy to hit "snooze" on their responsibilities.

2. The High-Pressure Environment
Between heavy project-based curriculums and the high cost of living, students face immense pressure. Procrastination doesn't just hurt grades; it wastes time that could be spent on part-time jobs or social well-being.

3. Lack of Immediate Stakes
Long-term goals (like passing an exam in three months) feel abstract. Without a near-term consequence for missing a study session today, students struggle to prioritize consistency over instant gratification.

4. Social Isolation in Productivity
Studying is often seen as a lonely chore. There is no platform that effectively merges the social connectivity students crave (like Instagram or Telegram) with the discipline they need to succeed.

USERS / TARGET MARKET
Primary users: Full-time tertiary and university students in Singapore, aged 17–25.

Key reasons this segment fits:
- Collaborative accountability: Friends are well-positioned to hold one another accountable.
- Mobile-first connectivity: Students rely on Telegram and Instagram. Embedding accountability into these digital spaces ensures interventions are immediate and visible.
- Reputation-driven behaviour: FoE transforms personal accountability into a shared social experience.

IDEA / SOLUTION

How It Works:
Users create a "Pact" consisting of:
- Task: e.g., "Study calculus for 2 hours."
- Deadline: Today, 8:00 PM – 10:00 PM.
- Verification Type: Photo, Video, Live Check, or AI-generated summary/reflection.

Pact Management:
- Active Limits: Users limited to a specific number of active Pacts at once (e.g., 3).
- History: Completed or resolved Pacts stored in user's history.

Verification Modes:
- Friend (The "Foe"): A human verifier. Even when using AI for initial check, the friend has the final say.
- AI Referee: Automated first pass for "solo" Pacts. AI verifies both task completion and consequences.

Social Features:
- "For You" page (similar to Instagram) where users track friends' progress.
- Integrated Consequences: Consequences handled within FoE app, not external platforms.
- Profile & Stats: Each user has a profile displaying consistency and success rates.

In-App Currency: Coins
- Earning: Users earn coins by completing tasks (scales with difficulty).
- Spending: Coins used to "buy" pre-defined consequences.
- Rewards: Currency redeemable for real-world vouchers (e.g., Grab, Foodpanda).

Stakes and Consequences:
- Pre-defined by the app to ensure they remain ethical, legal, and private.
- Only trigger if task is not completed by the deadline.

Why It Works:
- Public Commitment + Immediate Consequences = Loss Aversion
- Pre-commitment: When set in a "cold" rational state, consequences feel fair by deadline time.
- Eliminates "internal bargaining" that usually leads to failure.

CONSEQUENCE IDEAS

The "Social Blunder" (Feed-Based):
- The Public Apology: Post a video saying "I let my inner sloth win today."
- Unfiltered Confession: Post a photo of most cluttered/messy drawer.
- The "Roast Me" Prompt: AI generates a roasting caption; user posts it with a selfie.
- The Fan Club: User posts a 50-word tribute praising friend's productivity.

The "Effort Tax" (Action-Based):
- The Fitness Penalty: Record video doing 20 push-ups or 1-minute plank.
- The Hand-Written Note: Write "I will not procrastinate" 20 times on paper.
- The Knowledge Check: AI gives 5-question quiz on a random boring topic.

The "Wallet Hit" (In-App Currency):
- The Redistribution: Coins transferred to the Friend/Foe.
- The Burn: Portion of coins deleted from economy.
- The Voucher Freeze: Barred from redeeming coins for vouchers for 48 hours.

The "Profile Shame" (Status-Based):
- The "Chicken" Badge: "Failure" icon on profile for 3 days.
- The Blackout: Profile picture replaced by "Default Procrastinator" avatar.
- The Streak Breaker: Consistency Score halved instantly.

The "Silly/Witty" (Playful):
- The Lyricist: Record themselves singing chorus of a cheesy pop song.
- The Bad Fashion Statement: Post photo in most mismatched outfit.
- The AI Puppet: AI writes user's "Bio" for 24 hours.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Core Problem:
University students in Singapore have access to countless productivity apps, but they're still struggling with procrastination and cannot be consistent with their habits. There is a gap between what they plan to do and what they actually get done. This isn't just about grades — it's causing real stress, wasting time, and leaving them feeling behind.

Problem Details:

1. The Willpower Deficit
Most productivity apps assume self-discipline, which is exactly what procrastinating students don't have. Private apps have zero stakes — when you skip a day, nothing happens and no one knows.
Survey finding: 85% of respondents struggle to stay consistent. Mostly motivated by external pressure (deadlines, grades) rather than internal systems.

2. The Temporal Disconnect
Students struggle connecting today's actions with rewards months away. Studying for an exam 3 months out feels abstract vs. TikTok's instant dopamine. This is "temporal discounting" — the brain undervalues future rewards over immediate pleasure.
Result: Last-minute cramming, all-nighters, poor retention, and disrupted sleep.

3. The Pressure Paradox
Singapore's intense academic environment creates pressure that sometimes causes MORE procrastination. When stressed, it's easier to scroll social media than face a mountain of tasks.
Opportunity cost: Every procrastinated hour could have been part-time work, skill-building, or rest.

4. The Social Isolation of Productivity
Students are constantly connected socially but productivity is a solo journey. No platform combines social connectivity with structured accountability.
Survey: 73% think friend accountability would help; 68% are comfortable sharing habit progress with friends.

USER RESEARCH

Survey conducted via Google Forms with ~23 respondents from target segment (polytechnic and university students, Singapore, ages 17–25). Distributed via Telegram and Instagram to NUS, NTU, SMU, local polytechnics.

Key Findings:
- Over 80% struggle to stay consistent with habits (studying, exercising, reading).
- Procrastination linked to stress and poorer outcomes (Yeo and Chiam 2025).
- External pressure (deadlines, grades) drives students more than private reminders.
- Aligns with behavioral research on precommitment and loss aversion (The Decision Lab 2021).

Social Accountability:
- ~75% agreed friend accountability would improve consistency.
- Similar proportion comfortable sharing habit progress with friends in controlled environment.
- Supports FoE's core idea of involving friends directly in the accountability loop.

Temporal Discounting:
- Long-term academic rewards feel vague; apps like TikTok provide instant satisfaction.
- People heavily discount future rewards relative to immediate ones.
- Classic student statement: "I know it is important, but it does not feel urgent until the night before."
- FoE's near-term, pre-agreed consequences are meant to create pressure in this gap.

Preferred Consequences:
- Light, social, and effort-based penalties over large financial ones.
- Open to "social blunders," small physical tasks (push-ups), or status changes.
- Reluctant to risk losing significant real money.

Impact on Problem and Solution:
- Main problem: lack of enforced accountability and immediate stakes that matter emotionally.
- Social accountability and friend verification should be central to FoE.
- Library of predefined, safe, playful consequences (social, physical, status-based) rather than money penalties.
- Social-first, gamified interface with feed, profiles, and streaks fits how students already use mobile apps.

TECHNOLOGY RESEARCH

Platform Recommendation: Cross-platform mobile framework (Flutter or React Native)
- Supports single codebase for both iOS and Android.
- Familiar, responsive app experience close to social and productivity apps students already use.

Backend Approach: Serverless / managed backend
Enables:
- Store user profiles, pacts, and history of completed/failed pacts.
- Store media uploads (photos, short videos) for task completion and consequence evidence.
- Run background checks at deadlines to trigger consequences.
- Send push notifications for upcoming deadlines, pending verifications, pact outcomes.

AI and Verification:
- Use established APIs for text generation and basic classification/computer vision.
- Generate witty roast captions and tribute posts for social consequences.
- Check that written reflections meet a basic length and coherence threshold.
- Confirm simple video properties (duration); leave nuanced judgment to human friends.
- Advanced analysis (detailed pose tracking) out of scope for MVP.
- Early versions: friends as primary referees, AI as support layer.

Key Technical Challenges:
- Handling frequent small media uploads on mobile networks.
- Designing AI interactions that feel fair and are hard to exploit.
- Building permission and visibility controls so consequence posts stay within FoE.
- Communicating data retention and visibility for sensitive content.

COMPETITOR RESEARCH

Habitica:
- Treats tasks and habits as RPG elements; users gain XP by completing actions, lose health by failing.
- Large user base; gamification supports ongoing engagement.
- Main stakes are virtual; when users lose interest in the game layer, motivation drops.

Beeminder:
- Users define goals with "yellow brick road" of expected progress. If they "derail," real money is charged.
- Uses loss aversion very directly.
- Too stressful and expensive for many students' day-to-day academic tasks.

StickK:
- Commitment contracts: users set goals, stake money, nominate a referee.
- If they fail, money goes to charity, anti-charity, or a friend.
- Feels too formal and high-stakes for a student wanting help starting a report earlier.

How FoE Differentiates:
- Between game-like habit trackers and money-based commitment contracts.
- Adopts precommitment concepts from Beeminder/StickK but uses in-app coins and safe social/effort consequences.
- Uses gamification from Habitica (coins, badges, streaks) tied to real social stakes through friend referees.
- Focuses on students with time-bound, app-limited consequences (public apologies in feed, physical challenges, temporary profile changes).
- Gap: student-focused, socially enforced, non-monetary accountability app that feels like a social platform.

MARKET RESEARCH

Procrastination Context:
- High levels of procrastination among Singapore university students linked to stress, lower performance, reduced wellbeing (Yeo and Chiam 2025).
- Singapore has high smartphone use and internet access across young adults.
- Students heavily use mobile and social platforms: Instagram, TikTok, Telegram.
- Academic culture emphasizes performance, grades, competition.
- Students juggle part-time work, co-curricular activities, social commitments.

Target Market (Initial):
- Primary: University undergraduates in Singapore, ages ~17–25 at NUS, NTU, SMU, SIM, polytechnics.
- Tech-savvy, active on social platforms, frequent procrastinators.
- Motivated by peer perception and social validation, responsive to gamified structures.

Future Expansion:
- East and Southeast Asia: Hong Kong, South Korea, Japan.
- International student segments with similar pressures.

Business and Product Direction:
- Freemium model: core accountability features free.
- Premium tier: ~S$2.99–4.99/month — unlimited pacts, richer analytics, exclusive consequences, cosmetic upgrades.
- Partnerships with student-popular brands (food delivery, cafes) for sponsored rewards.
- Early growth via campus networks and friend referrals (each pact needs at least one "Foe").
- Long-term: FoE as accountability layer on top of calendars and to-do lists.

Evaluate whether the application effectively addresses:
1. Difficulty starting tasks due to high activation energy.
2. Internal bargaining ("I'll do it later") without real consequences.

Focus:
- Creating a pact
- Submitting evidence for a completed pact

Study Design: Two-day validation study with 6 participants.

Methodology:
- One-on-one interviews with 6 university students (ages 19–25).
- Participants tested clickable Figma prototype.
- All participants: self-identified as frequent procrastinators, had a task due within 48 hours they intended to complete today, reported experiencing guilt after delaying tasks.

DAY 1 – Pact Creation Session (20 minutes)
Procedure:
1. Navigate to pact creation screen, create a pact for identified task.
2. Set deadline as today.
3. Select Human verification.
4. Choose "Jun Wei" as accountability friend (for testing purposes).
5. Select any consequence they feel is appropriate.
Interview Questions: When do you plan to start? Do you think the pact will affect your behavior?
Simulated in-app reminders at: 6 hours remaining, 2 hours remaining, 1 hour remaining.

DAY 2 – Evidence Submission (20 minutes)
- Completed task: submit evidence through prototype.
- Did not complete task: simulate submitting evidence.
Interview Questions: What factors influenced when/whether you started? How was your experience? Did the pact affect your behavior? What made you pick your consequence?

Validation Limitations:
Short 24-hour timeframe evaluates short-term behavioral influence only. Longitudinal testing needed for sustained behavior change measurement.

KEY FINDINGS

A. Consequence Selection Behaviour
- All 6 participants selected relatively simple or low-severity consequences.
- None selected highly uncomfortable or financially meaningful consequences.
- Some said they "chose something I wouldn't mind doing."
- Interpretation: Users optimize for psychological comfort rather than effectiveness when left fully in control. Challenges assumption that users will self-impose sufficiently strong consequences.
- Recommendations:
  - Allow random or system-suggested consequences users only discover if they fail.
  - Have the "validator" (friend) set the consequence.

B. Task Completion Outcome
- 4/6 completed task within agreed timeframe.
- 2/6 did not complete.
- None stated consequence was an important factor in completion.
- Many admitted they chose easy consequences to avoid real discomfort.
- Interpretation: Strength of behavioral influence is dependent on consequence severity.
- Recommendation: Introduce stronger, socially or personally meaningful consequences.

C. Pact Creation Flow Increases Activation Energy
- Participants required to complete 10+ interactive steps to create a pact.
- 4/6 commented the process felt "long."
- 2/6 said they would not create a pact for small tasks due to effort required.
- Interpretation: High friction of setup violates "Flexibility and Efficiency of Use" heuristic. High severity issue — creates barrier at precisely the moment users are most likely to avoid the task.
- Recommendations:
  - Smart defaults and suggested options for task type, verification, and consequence.
  - "Quick pact" mode for small/low-stakes tasks.

NEXT STEPS
1. Redesign Pact Creation Flow:
   a. Allow new users to begin immediately with minimal decision-making.
   b. Allow advanced users to customize settings.
2. Improve consequence page — introduce "Random Consequence" feature.
3. Add additional features such as a coin shop.
4. Conduct user testing with 12 users with revised prototype.

DATA ANALYSIS SUMMARY

Task Completion Rate: 66.7% (4/6 completed).
Pact Creation Time:
- Average: 1.57 minutes.
- Despite being under 2 minutes, majority described process as "Long," "Lengthy," "Not suitable for simple tasks," "Unnecessarily heavy."

Consequence Severity Pattern:
- All 6 selected low-stakes consequences.
- 2 chose exercise, 2 chose to skip an activity, 1 chose social media detox, 1 chose Sloth badge.
- Common reasoning: "Didn't want something too harsh," "Played it safe," "Not too serious in case I failed."

Recommendations from Analysis:

1. Externalize Consequence Selection ("Mercy selection" issue):
- Implement "Mystery" or "Random" Consequences — users commit to severity level (low/medium/high) without knowing specific penalty until failure.
- Restores "psychological cost" of failure by removing user control over specific penalty.
- Eliminates internal bargaining that undermines behavioral change.

2. Streamline "Quick-Pact" Architecture:
- Fast Pact Creation: Simplified mode using Smart Defaults (e.g., default to "Selfie" verification and "10-minute" reminders).
- Progressive Disclosure: Hide advanced customization behind "Advanced" toggle for power users.
- Per Efficiency of Use heuristic: reducing "activation energy" ensures app is easier to use than task is to avoid.
